Time-Resolved Theta–Alpha Shift Dynamics in Development

Discriminating chirp from mixture hypotheses using cycle-level EEG burst analysis

Introduction

The dominant posterior alpha rhythm (~10 Hz in adults) emerges gradually during childhood, with peak alpha frequency (PAF) rising from approximately 6–7 Hz at age 5 to 10 Hz by late adolescence. In younger children, spectral analysis of resting-state EEG reveals a characteristic “theta shelf”—a broadened leftward shoulder of the alpha peak extending into the traditional theta band (4–7 Hz). This shelf collapses with age as the peak narrows and shifts toward higher frequencies.

Two competing hypotheses explain the theta shelf at the level of individual neural bursts:

Hypothesis A: Within-Burst Chirp

Individual alpha bursts sweep from theta to alpha frequencies within each burst (a “chirp”). When averaged across many bursts, this frequency sweep produces spectral smear between the theta and alpha bands. As the brain matures, bursts become more frequency-stable, reducing the chirp and collapsing the shelf.

Hypothesis B: Population Mixture

Distinct theta-frequency and alpha-frequency burst populations co-exist. In young children, theta bursts dominate; with development, the mixing ratio shifts toward alpha-frequency bursts. The shelf reflects the spectral overlap of two separate populations, not frequency instability within individual bursts.

These hypotheses are indistinguishable in the frequency domain—both produce the same broadened spectral peak. Discriminating them requires time-resolved, cycle-level analysis of burst dynamics. This study develops a simulation framework to evaluate which burst-detection methods can reliably distinguish these hypotheses, then applies the winning method to the Healthy Brain Network (HBN) developmental EEG dataset.

Methods

Forward Model

We constructed a NeuroDSP-based forward model generating realistic developmental EEG under each hypothesis. The model includes:

  • Aperiodic backbone: 1/f noise with knee at 1 Hz, exponent declining from 1.65 (age 5) to 1.35 (age 20), calibrated to empirical HBN resting-state data (n=1,933). The low knee frequency reproduces the empirical delta/theta power ratio (65%/21%).
  • Burst generation: Gaussian-windowed oscillatory bursts at ~2/s with age-dependent cycle counts (2–4 at age 5, up to 4–8 at age 20). Burst SNR ranges from −6 dB (age 5) to −1 dB (age 20) relative to background—negative values reflect that individual bursts are quieter than the aperiodic floor in the time domain but still contribute visible spectral peaks due to tonal concentration at specific frequencies.
  • Developmental PAF curve: Peak alpha frequency from 6.5 Hz (age 5) to 10.2 Hz (age 20), following the Freschl et al. (2022) meta-analytic trajectory.

Five generative regimes were implemented, each producing a distinct time-domain burst structure but designed so that Regimes 1 and 2 yield statistically indistinguishable average power spectra (the “theta shelf gate”):

  1. Chirp (Hypothesis A): A fraction of bursts (85% at age 5, declining to 10% at age 20) sweep linearly from 4 Hz to PAF(age); the remainder are frequency-stable at PAF.
  2. Mixture (Hypothesis B): Two burst populations—theta (4 Hz) and alpha (PAF)—with age-dependent mixing weights (85% theta at age 5, declining to 10% at age 20).
  3. Drift (control): Single oscillator at PAF with trial-to-trial jitter but no within-burst chirp.
  4. Co-occurrence: Theta and alpha bursts with 30% temporal overlap probability.
  5. Broadband narrowing: Single oscillator whose bandwidth narrows with age (3→1 Hz).

Candidate Methods

Six burst-detection and spectral decomposition methods were evaluated:

  1. Specparam (spectral parameterization): Number of peaks in the 4–12 Hz band.
  2. Threshold bursts (fBOSC-style): Amplitude thresholding after bandpass filtering.
  3. Bycycle (cycle-by-cycle): Mean period slope across detected burst cycles.
  4. itEMD + IF (intrinsic frequency): Mean instantaneous frequency slope within bursts.
  5. CDL (convolutional dictionary learning): Number of learned theta-alpha atoms.
  6. HMM (hidden Markov model): Number of oscillatory states with theta-alpha peaks.

Evaluation

Each method was applied to all 5 regimes × 6 ages × 5 random seeds at analysis-level SNR (5–12 dB). Discrimination was assessed via ROC AUC (chirp vs. mixture), Cohen’s d, and computational cost. Because single 2-second epochs proved insufficient for all methods (AUC ≤ 0.59), we additionally evaluated multi-epoch aggregation (30 simulated subjects × 50 epochs each).

Spectral estimation note: The parent HBN analysis pipeline uses wavelet-based spectral estimation (meeglet; Bw = 0.35 oct, 12 bins/octave), while this simulator uses Welch periodograms for interactive speed (<2s response). A quantitative comparison on identical simulated signals shows PSD shape correlation r ≈ 0.96 between methods—a meaningful difference reflecting the distinct frequency resolution properties of FFT-based vs. wavelet-based estimators. However, the surviving discrimination methods (bycycle period slopes, HMM state detection) operate entirely in the time domain and are independent of PSD estimation, so this discrepancy does not affect the scientific conclusions.

Why Bycycle Works

Bycycle’s period slope measures the systematic change in cycle duration within detected bursts. Under the chirp hypothesis, each burst traverses from lower to higher frequency, producing a monotonic decrease in period across consecutive cycles. Under the mixture hypothesis, each burst oscillates at a fixed frequency (either theta or alpha), so consecutive cycles have approximately equal periods—yielding low period slopes regardless of which population a burst belongs to.

This makes period slope a direct, time-domain readout of within-burst frequency structure that does not require spectral decomposition or source separation—avoiding the mode-mixing problems that invalidated itEMD for this application.

Robustness

Both bycycle and HMM maintained their discrimination performance under realistic artifact conditions (EOG blinks, EMG muscle noise, 60 Hz line noise, and all combined). AUC changes were <0.03 in all conditions, indicating that artifact contamination is not a confound for these methods.

HBN Empirical Results

The surviving methods were applied to the full Healthy Brain Network developmental EEG dataset (n = 1,885 subjects, ages 5–21, eyes-closed resting state). Posterior channels (Oz, lateral occipital, Pz) were averaged and all available epochs per subject were aggregated before computing burst statistics. Forty-five subjects with fewer than 10 usable epochs were excluded. All subjects passed the parent project’s QC pipeline (power-based and channel quality filters). Split-half reliability was assessed across 1,000 random splits.

Bycycle Period Slopes Decline with Age

Mean period slope decreased from 4.64 (ages 5–6) to 3.58 (ages 16–21), with Spearman ρ = −0.32 (95% CI: −0.36 to −0.27, p < 0.0001) and Cohen’s d = 0.63 (95% CI: 0.50–0.77). The median trajectory is monotonically decreasing across all six age bins (4.27→4.14→3.90→3.38→3.11→3.10). Split-half reliability of the age-bin trajectory was r = 0.93 (CI: 0.79–0.99). Higher period slopes in younger children indicate greater within-burst frequency variability—consistent with Hypothesis A, where a large fraction of bursts chirp from theta toward alpha frequencies. The effect holds equally in males (ρ = −0.32, d = 0.69, n = 1,236) and females (ρ = −0.31, d = 0.57, n = 649).

HMM Oscillatory States Converge with Age

The number of oscillatory HMM states (states with clear theta-alpha spectral peaks) declined from 1.35 (ages 5–6) to 1.13 (ages 16–21), with Spearman ρ = −0.41 (95% CI: −0.44 to −0.37, p < 0.0001) and Cohen’s d = 1.05 (95% CI: 0.91–1.21). Split-half reliability: r = 0.97 (CI: 0.91–1.00). Younger children exhibit broader spectral occupancy across multiple oscillatory regimes, while older children converge to a single stable alpha oscillator. This pattern is consistent with Hypothesis A: chirp-dominated bursts produce broader spectral occupancy that the HMM captures as multiple states, whereas frequency-stable bursts produce a single dominant state. The effect is sex-invariant (males ρ = −0.40, females ρ = −0.42).

Peak Alpha Frequency Trajectory

Specparam-extracted peak alpha frequency rose from 8.8 Hz (ages 5–6) to 9.7 Hz (ages 16–21), confirming the expected developmental PAF shift and validating the pipeline against the established literature. Cross-validation against the parent project’s meeglet-derived peak frequencies showed strong agreement (Pearson r = 0.81, mean absolute difference 0.4 Hz).

Sex Invariance

Both key findings replicate identically across sex. Males (n = 1,236) and females (n = 649) show overlapping developmental trajectories for bycycle period slope (M: ρ = −0.32, d = 0.69; F: ρ = −0.31, d = 0.57) and HMM oscillatory states (M: ρ = −0.40, d = 0.99; F: ρ = −0.42, d = 1.19). The correlation difference between sexes is <0.02 for both measures, confirming that the chirp-to-stable transition is a sex-invariant maturational process.

Discussion

Hypothesis Adjudication

The full-scale HBN analysis (n = 1,885) provides strong, reliable evidence favoring Hypothesis A (within-burst chirp) over Hypothesis B (population mixture). Two independent lines of evidence converge:

  1. Declining period slopes: Bycycle mean period slope decreases with age (ρ = −0.32, d = 0.63, 95% CI: 0.50–0.77), indicating that younger children’s bursts exhibit greater within-burst frequency change. This is the signature predicted by Hypothesis A: chirp-dominated bursts in early childhood transition to frequency-stable alpha bursts in adolescence.
  2. Converging oscillatory states: HMM analysis shows that younger children occupy more oscillatory states (ρ = −0.41, d = 1.05, 95% CI: 0.91–1.21), consistent with the broader spectral footprint produced by chirping bursts. Mature oscillators concentrate in a single alpha state.

Both findings are highly reliable (split-half r = 0.93 and 0.97 respectively) and sex-invariant (slope difference between males and females < 0.01 in correlation magnitude). Nonparametric effect sizes confirm the pattern (Cliff’s δ = 0.45 for bycycle, 0.57 for HMM). Under Hypothesis B, period slopes would be uniformly low at all ages (because both theta and alpha bursts are internally frequency-stable), and HMM state count would remain elevated into adolescence (reflecting persistent dual populations). Neither prediction is supported by the data.

The evidence favors Hypothesis A with medium-to-large effect sizes whose confidence intervals exclude zero. However, we cannot fully exclude a hybrid model. The residual period slope variability at older ages (SD = 1.81 for ages 16–21) leaves room for a minority chirp component persisting into adolescence, or for other sources of cycle-to-cycle frequency variation unrelated to either hypothesis (e.g., non-stationarity of PAF itself).

Method Insights

The key methodological finding is that cycle-by-cycle analysis (bycycle period slope) provides strong discrimination between chirp and mixture hypotheses in simulation, while methods relying on spectral decomposition (CDL, specparam) or modal decomposition (itEMD) fail. This reflects a fundamental property of the problem: the two hypotheses produce indistinguishable spectra by construction, so any method that reduces the signal to its spectral content cannot discriminate them.

Limitations

  • Simulation circularity: Methods were validated on the same simulation framework used to design them. The empirical results partially mitigate this concern—the predicted age trajectory holds in real data—but unmeasured confounds could produce similar patterns for different reasons.
  • Multi-epoch requirement: Reliable discrimination requires aggregation across many epochs. Younger subjects tend to have fewer usable epochs, which could differentially affect precision across age bins. However, split-half reliability (r = 0.93–0.97) indicates that per-bin estimates are stable even when halving the sample.
  • Effect size interpretation: The bycycle effect (d = 0.63) is smaller than in simulation (d = 1.8–2.9). This gap likely reflects the higher SNR used for method evaluation, mixed-model burst populations in real data, and biological variability not captured by the simulation. The HMM effect (d = 1.05) is notably larger, suggesting that state-space approaches may be more sensitive to the underlying phenomenon in empirical data.
  • Hybrid possibility: Real developmental EEG likely exhibits elements of both hypotheses. The declining but nonzero period slopes at older ages are consistent with a small residual chirp component coexisting with predominantly stable alpha bursts.
  • Age-bin imbalance: The 16–21 bin has fewer subjects (n = 128) than younger bins (n = 307–458), reflecting HBN’s recruitment demographics. Wider confidence intervals in this bin are expected but do not undermine the overall trajectory.

Conclusion

Converging evidence from simulation validation and full-scale empirical application (n = 1,885) supports Hypothesis A: the developmental theta shelf arises primarily from within-burst frequency chirp rather than from a mixture of distinct theta and alpha burst populations. Bycycle period slopes decline with age in HBN data (d = 0.63, 95% CI: 0.50–0.77), indicating that younger children’s alpha bursts sweep across a wider frequency range. HMM oscillatory states converge toward a single alpha mode with maturation (d = 1.05, 95% CI: 0.91–1.21). Both findings are highly reliable (split-half r > 0.93) and sex-invariant.

The practical implication is that the theta shelf is not a separate oscillatory component requiring independent neural generators. Rather, it is a spectral consequence of immature frequency control within the alpha-generating network. As thalamocortical circuits mature, burst frequency stabilizes, the chirp diminishes, and the shelf collapses into the narrow adult alpha peak.

These findings suggest that developmental EEG research should move beyond band-power summaries toward cycle-level characterizations of oscillatory dynamics. Tools like bycycle period slope (<0.001 s/epoch, robust to artifacts) make this tractable at scale.
