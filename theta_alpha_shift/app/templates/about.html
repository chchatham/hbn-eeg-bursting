Time-Resolved Theta–Alpha Shift Dynamics in Development

Discriminating chirp from mixture hypotheses using cycle-level EEG burst analysis

Introduction

The dominant posterior alpha rhythm (~10 Hz in adults) emerges gradually during childhood, with peak alpha frequency (PAF) rising from approximately 6–7 Hz at age 5 to 10 Hz by late adolescence. In younger children, spectral analysis of resting-state EEG reveals a characteristic “theta shelf”—a broadened leftward shoulder of the alpha peak extending into the traditional theta band (4–7 Hz). This shelf collapses with age as the peak narrows and shifts toward higher frequencies.

Two competing hypotheses explain the theta shelf at the level of individual neural bursts:

Hypothesis A: Within-Burst Chirp

Individual alpha bursts sweep from theta to alpha frequencies within each burst (a “chirp”). When averaged across many bursts, this frequency sweep produces spectral smear between the theta and alpha bands. As the brain matures, bursts become more frequency-stable, reducing the chirp and collapsing the shelf.

Hypothesis B: Population Mixture

Distinct theta-frequency and alpha-frequency burst populations co-exist. In young children, theta bursts dominate; with development, the mixing ratio shifts toward alpha-frequency bursts. The shelf reflects the spectral overlap of two separate populations, not frequency instability within individual bursts.

These hypotheses are indistinguishable in the frequency domain—both produce the same broadened spectral peak. Discriminating them requires time-resolved, cycle-level analysis of burst dynamics. This study develops a simulation framework to evaluate which burst-detection methods can reliably distinguish these hypotheses, then applies the winning method to the Healthy Brain Network (HBN) developmental EEG dataset.

Methods

Forward Model

We constructed a NeuroDSP-based forward model generating realistic developmental EEG under each hypothesis. The model includes:

  • Aperiodic backbone: 1/f noise with knee at 5 Hz, exponent declining from 1.65 (age 5) to 1.35 (age 20), calibrated to empirical HBN resting-state data (n=1,933).
  • Burst generation: Gaussian-windowed oscillatory bursts at ~3/s with 2–5 cycles each, SNR of 8 dB relative to background.
  • Developmental PAF curve: Peak alpha frequency from 6.5 Hz (age 5) to 10.2 Hz (age 20), following the Freschl et al. (2022) meta-analytic trajectory.

Five generative regimes were implemented, each producing a distinct time-domain burst structure but designed so that Regimes 1 and 2 yield statistically indistinguishable average power spectra (the “theta shelf gate”):

  1. Chirp (Hypothesis A): Each burst sweeps linearly from 5 Hz to PAF(age).
  2. Mixture (Hypothesis B): Two burst populations—theta (5 Hz) and alpha (PAF)—with age-dependent mixing weights.
  3. Drift (control): Single oscillator at PAF with trial-to-trial jitter but no within-burst chirp.
  4. Co-occurrence: Theta and alpha bursts with 30% temporal overlap probability.
  5. Broadband narrowing: Single oscillator whose bandwidth narrows with age (3→1 Hz).

Candidate Methods

Six burst-detection and spectral decomposition methods were evaluated:

  1. Specparam (spectral parameterization): Number of peaks in the 4–12 Hz band.
  2. Threshold bursts (fBOSC-style): Amplitude thresholding after bandpass filtering.
  3. Bycycle (cycle-by-cycle): Mean period slope across detected burst cycles.
  4. itEMD + IF (intrinsic frequency): Mean instantaneous frequency slope within bursts.
  5. CDL (convolutional dictionary learning): Number of learned theta-alpha atoms.
  6. HMM (hidden Markov model): Number of oscillatory states with theta-alpha peaks.

Evaluation

Each method was applied to all 5 regimes × 6 ages × 5 random seeds. Discrimination was assessed via ROC AUC (chirp vs. mixture), Cohen’s d, and computational cost. Because single 2-second epochs proved insufficient for all methods (AUC ≤ 0.59), we additionally evaluated multi-epoch aggregation (30 simulated subjects × 50 epochs each).

Why Bycycle Works

Bycycle’s period slope measures the systematic change in cycle duration within detected bursts. Under the chirp hypothesis, each burst traverses from lower to higher frequency, producing a monotonic decrease in period across consecutive cycles. Under the mixture hypothesis, each burst oscillates at a fixed frequency (either theta or alpha), so consecutive cycles have approximately equal periods—yielding low period slopes regardless of which population a burst belongs to.

This makes period slope a direct, time-domain readout of within-burst frequency structure that does not require spectral decomposition or source separation—avoiding the mode-mixing problems that invalidated itEMD for this application.

Robustness

Both bycycle and HMM maintained their discrimination performance under realistic artifact conditions (EOG blinks, EMG muscle noise, 60 Hz line noise, and all combined). AUC changes were <0.03 in all conditions, indicating that artifact contamination is not a confound for these methods.

Discussion

The key finding is that cycle-by-cycle analysis (bycycle period slope) provides strong discrimination between chirp and mixture hypotheses in simulation, while methods relying on spectral decomposition (CDL, specparam) or modal decomposition (itEMD) fail. This reflects a fundamental property of the problem: the two hypotheses produce indistinguishable spectra by construction, so any method that reduces the signal to its spectral content cannot discriminate them.

Several limitations constrain interpretation:

  • Simulation circularity: Methods are evaluated on the same simulation framework used to design them. Real EEG may contain features not captured by our model that differentially affect methods.
  • Multi-epoch requirement: Reliable discrimination requires ~50 epochs (100 seconds of data). Subjects with fewer clean epochs will have noisier estimates.
  • Age 5 weakness: At the youngest age, PAF (6.5 Hz) is very close to the chirp start frequency (5.0 Hz), leaving little frequency range for chirp. Discrimination is weaker here (AUC = 0.66).
  • Hybrid possibility: Real developmental EEG may exhibit elements of both hypotheses—chirp within some bursts and mixture across populations. Bycycle period slope would show intermediate values in this case.

Implications for HBN Analysis

For Stage 2 application to HBN data: if mean period slope decreases systematically with age (as chirp range shrinks), this supports Hypothesis A. If period slope is uniformly low across ages, this supports Hypothesis B (fixed-frequency bursts at all ages). Mixed results would suggest a hybrid model or that neither simple hypothesis fully captures the developmental trajectory.

Conclusion

Bycycle period slope is a computationally efficient (<0.001 s/epoch), robust, and highly discriminative (AUC > 0.95) indicator of within-burst frequency chirp in simulated developmental EEG. It is the recommended primary method for testing whether the theta-alpha shelf in HBN data arises from within-burst chirp (Hypothesis A) or population mixture (Hypothesis B). HMM state analysis provides secondary confirmatory evidence. Application to the full HBN dataset will determine which hypothesis best accounts for the observed developmental trajectory.
